Kabir Arora

Main

Living is a process of prediction, of appropriately weighting past information to inform meaningful future behavior. This predictive behavior can be analyzed as a sequence of discrete events in time, in terms of co-occurrences or dependencies between individual elements. Previous work has demonstrated a sweeping presence of long-range dependencies (LRDs) in a variety of biological behaviors—a finding indicative of underlying hierarchical mechanisms. However, more profound is the idea that this structure is a natural consequence of a sequence produced by a predictive system.

To target this potential, I plan to investigate the utility of predictive structure in the communicative behavior of European starlings. Communication between organisms relies on co-evolved perceptual and generative mechanisms, and thus innate structure in generative processes are likely to be mirrored in perceptual processes.

I will curate a song dataset from two starlings, and train a structured state space sequence model (S4) individually on each singer. I will train starlings on a vocal recognition task with the natural song, and then introduce song generated by S4. Usefully, S4 explicitly models LRDs in its internal state, which I can isolate to perform a controlled perturbation to the artificial song’s long-range structure content, biasing it towards short-range structure. Discrimination performance with the long-range degraded song will be used to determine perceptual salience of LRDs in vocal recognition.

Ultimately this project aims to utilize modern sequence models to test for any natural, perceptual mechanism of LRDs in vocal recognition.
